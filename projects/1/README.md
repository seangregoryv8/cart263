# PROJECT 1 ARTIST STATEMENT: LIGHTSCRIPT

For this project, I wanted to deviate from normal JavaScript projects I normally do. Most have a story (beginning, middle, and end), some level of detail towards smaller elements and usually have a finite, finishing loop with it. For a project like this, with a simple description: make use of JavaScript to interact with the Document Object Model (DOM), I wanted to try something more unique. I thought of its core statements, talks of simple DOM interaction. I had wondered on the idea for a long while, and thought about how I could take such a simple concept and expand upon it further.

In the process of designing the original idea, I became interested in how very simple systems can evolve into complex creative spaces when multiple users interact within the system. I started researching similar ideas around crowdsourcing and the cooperative development of digital spaces. One of the projects that resonated with me was r/place , a mass online participatory art experiment, which captured the imaginations of many enthusiasts, led to the formation of many smaller communities, and resulted in an intricate continuous piece of pixel art (one that I actually got to experience first hand). Even though the task of clicking one pixel was simple, the final artwork was extensive, detailed and created as a result of the combined effort of thousands of users. An article published by ‘The Conversation’ cites that this project was a ‘viral demonstration of individual contributions, randomness and community shaping, and how a small interface can be transformed into a massive pixel-piece ’. We actively saw factions get formed based on different cliques and interests, many of them painting their own flags, only to get outshined by various others attempting to draw art from their favourite internet niche (alongside Canadians showing the internet that they were not able to draw a maple leaf).

Another example of this that shaped my thinking, was The Million Dollar Homepage , which was an internet experiment devised by Alex Tew in 2005. The site was a 1000x1000 pixel grid (with each pixel being sold for a single dollar) and soon created not only a fifty-thousand by fifty-thousand-pixel collage of hundreds of market stalls, but a million-dollar site in the process. While initially an experiment in marketing, this also has a very obvious digital collaging aspect, in that it is a constructed artwork, accumulated by the input of a wide range of disparate individuals. Aside from these, theorising around digital media also had an impact. For example, in The Language of New Media , Lev Manovich describes how digital media systems often focus on interactivity and modularity allowing the user to drag, drop and reorganise the components of a piece of software. The user is not only interacting with the digital system, but they are also having an effect on the result. This was very similar to what I wished to do, I did not want to produce a completed work of art, but rather I wanted to make a system where the user could build visual elements that were fluid and meandered around.

In researching similar ideas, I also came across the Poietic Generator , an early example of collaborative digital art developed in the 1980s. The Poietic Generator allowed multiple participants to simultaneously modify a shared visual grid, creating evolving patterns in real time. Unlike traditional artworks created by a single artist, the system emphasized collective authorship, where the final visual result emerged from the actions of many participants interacting within the same system. This project demonstrated that digital networks could enable entirely new forms of artistic collaboration and collective creativity.

Another concept intrigued me from these readings, the idea of a little sandbox for you to make and create whatever you wanted. When I was younger, I played a lot of those kinds of simulations, notably Garry’s Mod, taking Half Life 2 assets and allowing you to craft your own stories with them. It’s since become one of the most well-known sandbox games in existence, solely by how open source it could get.

I decided to use some of those readings, alongside my knowledge of JavaScript, to create something similar. I wanted to allow the user to make and control various “assets” via a command window that would bring them up, similarly to the Dev Tools on websites or the Command Line in some video games, basically acting like debug tools. I started by making a base command prompt, complete with green text, a textarea, and a submit button that would intake commands and figure out what to do with them. I tied the submit button to an addEventListener, proceeded to take whatever was written in the “command line” textarea, and, after weeding out what happens if nothing is given, could do whatever I wanted with it.

This project makes extensive use of switch statements, essentially massive if else statements, proven to be more effective given multiple possible answers, as it searches for it directly instead of doing “if this, no then if this, no then if this” until it reaches what could possibly be the bottom answer. I designed two switch statements, one for multi-worded answers, and one for single-worded answers, and got to work. I first stated serious, designing a cube creation statement with multiple sub-clauses within, which summons a cube on screen. You can specify its width, length, x and y values, or just give it plain values. Afterwards, I designed cube manipulation, where you could choose where to move it on the window or how much to rotate it. Once I had that solid foundation, I decided to recycle some pre-existing code I had just written and tied down keys to those same movements. WASD moves a selected cube, QE rotates it. But then I needed to design a way to select cubes. Simple enough, to highlight it in a certain colour and give feedback, followed by reversing the order to deselect it, both of which I not only allowed you to input into the console, but also use MOUSE0 to control. Other cube-related instances would be needed, such as a “cubes” command to spawn multiple, “rename” to change its name that it would present with, “list” for the console to list them out, “delete” to remove a selected cube, and “erase” to remove every single cube. Additionally, I added a “clear” feature that erases the console, and a “reset” that simply reloads the page.

Then, deciding that it should not just be a plain console, I began to give it an actual personality, inspired by some works I saw about bots from the class. I gave it a long list of codes that would show it acting smug, annoyed, angry, and so on, to give me a somewhat infinite amount of code I could reach with that. I had really wanted to tie an AI to it for infinitely generating answers, but that would go beyond the limits of the small scale I really wanted for this.

A big thing I wanted to look into, and the portion that was the hardest to get right, was persistent storage. I’ve used localStorage before, and knew how it all functioned, so I decided to really implement the crowd sourcing thing to make everyone feel like artists for this individual project (at least until I can figure out a wider range of things to do).
While the final system functions as intended, it remains somewhat clunky to use. However, this was intentional. Command-line systems are rarely designed for convenience (the invention of the GUI is proof of that). Instead, they reward users who take the time to learn how they function. Arch Linux is the hardest OS to learn solely because it is entirely done within CLI. By maintaining this slightly awkward interface, I hoped to create a sense that users who invest time into understanding the system gain greater control over the environment.

I would love to add more features into this project, and thanks to the infrastructure and manner in which I created it, I can absolutely add more in the future if ideas strike me. So long as it remains built entirely off the idea of the CLI, maintaining that secret hacker-like atmosphere, it would work well in this.
Overall, this project explores the idea of creative experimentation by the baseline parts fine-tuned to a diamond-level precision. This project transforms a basic web page into a small interactive sandbox. Influenced by collaborative digital projects such as r/place, The Million Dollar Homepage, and the Poietic Generator, this reflects the idea that, rather than presenting a fixed artwork, let someone else use their own imagination to construct their masterpiece. Provide the brush and let them paint. In this way, LightScript demonstrates how even simple DOM interactions can be expanded into systems that encourage exploration, creativity, and user-driven interaction.
